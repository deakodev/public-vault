My dissertation investigates how clinicians interact with decision support tools during time-critical workflows — specifically the mismatch between what a system surfaces and what a provider actually attends to.

## The core question

Clinical decision support (CDS) tools are designed to reduce error by surfacing relevant information at the point of care. The implicit assumption is that information surfaced = information processed. This is almost certainly wrong in a busy clinical environment, and it matters: alert fatigue, workflow disruption, and automation bias are all downstream of this gap.

I want to understand the attentional dynamics of CDS use. Not just whether clinicians click through alerts, but what cognitive state they're in when they do, what competing demands are active, and how interface design mediates the interaction between the alert and the provider's existing mental model.

## Methods

- Contextual inquiry and think-aloud protocols in clinical settings
- Eye-tracking studies to measure actual attention allocation during simulated high-acuity scenarios
- Design intervention: iterating on alert presentation and measuring downstream reasoning quality

## Theoretical grounding

The work draws on joint cognitive systems theory (Woods, Hollnagel), signal detection theory, and attentional resource models. The goal is not to add another empirical finding to the pile but to develop a design framework that accounts for cognitive context — not just information availability.

## Status

Currently in the study design and IRB phase. Pilot data collection expected mid-2026.
